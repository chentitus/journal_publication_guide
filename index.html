A guide for authors

What gets a paper rejected here is rarely the topic.

Reviewers seldom doubt that a subject is worth studying. They doubt that a manuscript has turned a subject into an argument. Read across our recent decisions and the same nine problems recur — and most rejected papers stumble on several at once.

Distilled from roughly two dozen recent editorial decisions: desk rejections, post-review rejections, and major-revision invitations.

The pattern

Reviewers tend to reject the same things.

A referee report can look like a long list of particular objections. Read enough of them and the particulars resolve into a short, stable set of underlying problems — the nine below. They are arranged by where in the process each one usually stops a manuscript: the first three rarely survive the editor's desk; the next three are what referees flag; the last three more often send a paper back for revision than end it outright.

These notes are anonymised and generalised across many manuscripts. No paper, author, or referee is identifiable — what remains is the pattern, and what each problem is really asking you to fix before you submit.

Stage 1

Before review

Why a paper never reaches a referee
I

The puzzle

Very common

A topic is not a question.

What reviewers say

“The paper describes its subject but never identifies a puzzle it sets out to resolve — it reads as a survey, not an inquiry.”

A question answerable by description alone generates no expectation to test. Reviewers can't see what the analysis adds beyond what is already known, so the paper stops at the editor's desk.

Before you submit

Open with a genuine tension that existing explanations leave unresolved, and make the whole paper the attempt to resolve it.

II

The framework

Very common

An organizing scheme is not an explanatory theory.

What reviewers say

“Several theories are invoked but never reconciled or applied; the framework sorts cases rather than explaining them, and adds little beyond established theory.”

A framework that only organizes produces no causal expectations to test; one that's introduced and then never used is decoration. Either way, the theory does no work.

Before you submit

Commit to one coherent logic, show what propositions it generates, apply it from introduction to conclusion, and say what it explains that existing theory cannot.

III

The concept

Very common

Undefined, unoperationalized, or conflated.

What reviewers say

“The key term is never defined — or is quietly conflated with an adjacent concept it is not — so it's impossible to tell how it was measured.”

An undefined core concept makes every claim resting on it impossible to falsify; and conflating it with a neighbouring idea invites the most damaging objection of all — that you are studying something other than what you claim.

Before you submit

Define each load-bearing concept where it first appears, show how it's identified in your material, and guard the boundary with the concepts it could be mistaken for.

Stage 2

In review

What referees flag
IV

The contribution

Very common

What is new, and why does it matter?

What reviewers say

“The evidence is solid, but what here is new? Much of this confirms what we already knew, or repeats a move others have made.”

Solidity alone earns no recommendation. A paper that doesn't situate itself against its closest neighbours — including the author's own earlier work — reads as a summary, and blurring new from previously published raises self-overlap concerns at the same time.

Before you submit

State your contribution in one plain sentence, early and again at the payoff, and situate it explicitly against the closest existing work.

V

The evidence

Common

Asserted, not shown.

What reviewers say

“The claim that X drove Y is asserted but never demonstrated; the evidence is scattered, and the obvious primary sources go unused.”

Causal verbs — catalysed, drove, shaped — invite scrutiny of the mechanism. If a concurrent event could explain the same outcome, or the documents a specialist expects are missing, the section collapses however plausible it reads.

Before you submit

For each causal claim, show the mechanism and rule out the obvious rival; anchor generalizations to specific sources; use the primary evidence the field expects; and keep your certainty within the data.

VI

The literature

Very common

Who are you in conversation with?

What reviewers say

“The review is too narrow — a few authors standing in for whole fields, the most relevant recent work missing, key sources cited then dropped.”

Missing the obvious counter-argument signals a gap or a one-sided case; a review built from policy reports rather than scholarship doesn't enter the conversation the journal serves.

Before you submit

Map the real debate, reach the last decade, engage the work that would challenge you — and use sources analytically rather than listing them.

In revision

What sends a paper back
VII

The design

Occasional · usually decisive

Can the design bear the claim?

What reviewers say

“The sample is too small for a model this complex; the case differs from the phenomenon theorized, so the central causal link cannot be identified.”

When the design can't support the weight placed on it — overfitting, weak generalizability, a case–theory mismatch — reviewers question the study itself, not merely its details. That is the hardest objection to fix in revision.

Before you submit

Match your claims to what your sample, case, and method can establish, and address validity and identification head-on rather than in a closing caveat.

VIII

Scope & balance

Common

Too much, or too one-sided.

What reviewers say

“The paper covers too much ground to evidence any of it — or it reads as a defence rather than an analysis, engaging only what flatters its conclusion.”

Breadth reads as ambition in a proposal and as thinness in a finished article. And a one-sided case — or a loaded binary like “the West” versus “developing countries” — undermines trust in the analysis.

Before you submit

Narrow to the argument you can prove and label the rest as future work; give the strongest opposing case a fair hearing.

IX

The craft

Common · compounds everything

The last mile.

What reviewers say

“The data are out of date, several passages need rewriting, references are missing or mis-styled, and a new idea appears for the first time in the conclusion.”

Avoidable flaws make a strong argument look unfinished and hand a hesitant reviewer easy reasons to say no. The last mile is where goodwill is won or lost.

Before you submit

Refresh time-sensitive data, proofread hardest what you wrote last, conform to the journal's citation style, and introduce nothing important in the conclusion.

One honest caveat

No guide guarantees acceptance.

But a manuscript that clears the first six of these before submission, and is polished against the last three, rarely fails for the reasons above — which frees a reviewer to engage what you actually have to say rather than what you forgot to do. That is the difference between a desk rejection and a fair hearing.
